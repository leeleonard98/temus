# Diversification

Diversification is the practice of spreading investments across many different
assets so that no single bad outcome can sink the entire portfolio. The
underlying intuition is that the future is uncertain: we do not know which
country, sector, or company will lead returns over the next decade, so we
own broad exposure and capture the average instead of betting on a winner.

## Why it works

Mathematically, the variance of a portfolio is not the simple average of the
variances of its holdings — it depends on how those holdings move together,
their covariance. When two assets are imperfectly correlated, combining them
reduces total volatility for any given level of expected return. This is
sometimes called "the only free lunch in finance" because you give up
nothing in expected return for the lower risk, provided the assets have
similar long-run return profiles.

A classic example: a portfolio of 60% global equities and 40% high-quality
government bonds historically had roughly two-thirds of the volatility of a
100% equity portfolio while delivering most of the return. Equities and
high-grade bonds tend to fall and rise at different times, especially in
recessions, so the bond sleeve cushions equity drawdowns.

## How to diversify

True diversification operates on multiple axes:

- **Asset class** — equities, bonds, cash, real estate, commodities.
- **Geography** — domestic vs. international developed vs. emerging.
- **Sector** — technology, healthcare, financials, energy, consumer.
- **Style** — large-cap vs. small-cap, value vs. growth.
- **Time** — dollar-cost averaging spreads entry points across cycles.

A common pitfall is "false diversification": owning twenty US technology
stocks looks like a diversified portfolio but in practice they are all
exposed to the same macro factor (US tech earnings and discount rates) and
will fall together in a sector drawdown. A single broad world-equity index
fund typically delivers more genuine diversification than a hand-picked
basket of twenty individual names.

## Limits

Diversification reduces idiosyncratic risk (the risk specific to a company
or sector) but cannot eliminate systematic risk (the risk that affects
every asset, like a global recession or a sharp rise in real interest
rates). In a true crisis correlations move toward 1 — almost everything
falls together — and the only diversifier that consistently helps is high
quality government debt or cash.

For most retail wealth-management clients, a globally diversified mix of
low-cost index funds, sized to their risk tolerance, captures the bulk of
the diversification benefit without the cost or complexity of more exotic
approaches.
