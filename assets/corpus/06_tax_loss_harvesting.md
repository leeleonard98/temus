# Tax-Loss Harvesting

Tax-loss harvesting (TLH) is the deliberate realisation of investment
losses in a taxable account to offset realised gains and, in some
jurisdictions, a limited amount of ordinary income. Used systematically it
can lift after-tax returns by a meaningful margin without changing the
underlying market exposure of the portfolio.

## Mechanics

When a holding has fallen below its cost basis, the investor sells it,
realising a capital loss. That loss is then netted against capital gains
realised elsewhere in the same tax year. Excess losses can typically be
carried forward indefinitely. To preserve the original market exposure the
proceeds are immediately reinvested in a similar — but not "substantially
identical" — security.

In the United States the **wash-sale rule** prevents claiming the loss if
the same or a substantially identical security is repurchased within 30
days before or after the sale. The standard workaround is to swap into a
fund that tracks a different but highly correlated index — for example,
selling an S&P 500 ETF and buying a total-US-market ETF, or rotating
between two MSCI World trackers from different issuers.

Other jurisdictions have analogous rules with different cooling-off
periods (UK: 30-day "bed and breakfast" rule; Canada: 30-day superficial
loss rule). Always check local rules before harvesting.

## Where it adds the most value

The benefits scale with:

- **Marginal tax rate** — higher-rate taxpayers benefit more.
- **Realised gains elsewhere** — losses are most valuable when there are
  gains to offset.
- **Volatility** — more frequent dispersion across holdings creates more
  harvesting opportunities, which is why direct indexing (owning the
  individual constituents of an index rather than the index fund) can
  unlock far more loss harvesting than holding a single ETF.
- **Time horizon** — the deferred tax compounds over the life of the
  portfolio.

Studies of direct-indexing TLH typically find an after-tax alpha of
roughly 1–2% per year in the early years of a portfolio, decaying as the
unrealised loss inventory shrinks.

## Pitfalls

Common mistakes include:

- **Triggering wash sales accidentally** — through automatic dividend
  reinvestment or trades in a spouse's account.
- **Locking in higher future tax** — TLH lowers the cost basis of the
  replacement holding, increasing the eventual gain on sale. The benefit
  is real but smaller than the headline savings; it is a tax *deferral*,
  not a tax elimination, unless the holding is ultimately gifted or
  passed via a step-up in basis.
- **Letting the tax tail wag the dog** — selling a holding purely for the
  loss when the strategic case for owning it remains is fine; selling a
  diversified holding to harvest a loss and then failing to replace it
  with a comparable instrument leaves the client out of the market.

A disciplined TLH process — typically run monthly or after large
drawdowns — pairs naturally with the rebalancing process and the
calendar of capital-gain realisations a wealth manager already runs.
