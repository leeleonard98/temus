# Rebalancing

Rebalancing is the process of returning a portfolio to its strategic asset
allocation after market movements have pushed the actual weights away from
target. It is a mechanical discipline that enforces "sell high, buy low"
behaviour and is one of the few sources of incremental return available to
a passive investor without taking active risk.

## Why rebalance

Without rebalancing, a portfolio's risk profile drifts with the market.
A 60/40 equity/bond portfolio left untouched through a strong equity bull
run can easily become 75/25 — meaning the investor is now taking far more
risk than the policy mix prescribed, often right before the cycle turns.
Conversely, after a sharp equity drawdown the same portfolio can drift to
50/50, leaving the investor under-exposed to the recovery.

Rebalancing solves both problems. By trimming the asset class that has
risen and topping up the one that has fallen, it locks in some gains from
the winner and buys the loser at lower prices. Empirically this can add
0.1% to 0.4% of annualised return for a typical multi-asset portfolio,
plus the more important benefit of keeping the actual risk profile aligned
with the policy.

## Strategies

There are three common strategies:

- **Calendar-based** — rebalance on a fixed schedule, e.g. annually or
  semi-annually. Simple and predictable. Quarterly is usually too frequent
  and increases transaction costs without improving the outcome.
- **Threshold-based** — rebalance whenever an asset-class weight deviates
  by more than X percentage points (often 5%) or X% (often 25%) from
  target. More responsive to volatility, captures more of the rebalancing
  premium, but harder to operationalise without monitoring.
- **Hybrid** — check on a calendar, rebalance only if thresholds are
  breached. The pragmatic default for most wealth-management practices.

## Practical considerations

Several details matter in implementation:

- **Tax** — selling winners in a taxable account triggers capital gains.
  Where possible, rebalance using new contributions or withdrawals,
  redirect dividends, and use tax-deferred accounts for the rebalancing
  trades.
- **Transaction costs** — bid-ask spreads and commissions can erode the
  rebalancing premium for small portfolios. Threshold rebalancing helps
  avoid trading on noise.
- **Bands** — using a tolerance band rather than rebalancing all the way
  to target reduces unnecessary turnover.
- **Trend overrides** — some practitioners add a "let winners run" rule
  that delays rebalancing during strong trends. The evidence is mixed; for
  most clients the simpler discipline outperforms over a full cycle.

A simple, default rebalancing policy for a private client portfolio is:
review at least annually; rebalance whenever any asset class is more than
5 percentage points off target; use new cash flows preferentially; harvest
losses where available before realising gains.
