# Risk Tolerance

Risk tolerance is the level of investment loss a client can absorb without
abandoning their plan. It has two distinct components — capacity for loss
(an objective measure of how much risk the client's finances can withstand)
and willingness to take risk (a subjective, emotional measure of how much
loss the client can stomach without selling) — and the binding constraint
is whichever of the two is lower.

## Capacity vs. willingness

**Capacity** is a balance-sheet question. A client with twenty years until
retirement, secure earned income, an emergency fund, and no leveraged
liabilities has high capacity for loss: a 40% equity drawdown at age 45
will recover before they need the money. A client three years from
retirement with most of their wealth in the portfolio has low capacity —
the same drawdown could permanently impair their standard of living.

**Willingness** is a psychology question. Some clients with high capacity
will still panic-sell at the bottom; some with low capacity have an
unusually steady disposition. Failing to take willingness seriously
produces the worst possible outcome: a portfolio sized to the client's
capacity but abandoned at the worst moment, locking in losses and missing
the recovery.

## Assessing it

Standard tooling includes:

- **Risk-tolerance questionnaires (RTQs)** — twenty-or-so multiple-choice
  items asking how the client would react to hypothetical scenarios. Useful
  but vulnerable to overstating tolerance during bull markets.
- **Drawdown stress tests** — show the client how their proposed portfolio
  performed in 2008, 2020, and similar episodes, and ask if they would
  actually have held through that drawdown without selling.
- **Goals-based framing** — ask not "how much loss can you handle?" but
  "if your retirement income fell from $X to $0.7X, would you still
  consider the plan a success?"
- **Conversations over time** — a single questionnaire is a snapshot;
  observed behaviour during real volatility is the truth.

## Mapping to a portfolio

A common mapping (illustrative):

- **Conservative** — 20–35% equities. Capital preservation, low volatility.
- **Balanced** — 40–60% equities. Long-term growth with meaningful bond
  cushion.
- **Growth** — 65–80% equities. Multi-decade horizon, high tolerance for
  drawdowns.
- **Aggressive** — 85%+ equities, possibly with leverage or concentrated
  bets. Suitable only for clients with both very high capacity and proven
  willingness.

## Reviewing it

Risk tolerance is not static. Major life events (marriage, children,
inheritance, redundancy) shift capacity overnight. Major market events
shift willingness, often suddenly and in ways the client did not predict.
A good wealth manager re-runs the assessment at least annually and
whenever a client's circumstances or stated comfort changes.
