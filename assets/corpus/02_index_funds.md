# Index Funds

An index fund is a pooled investment vehicle — a mutual fund or
exchange-traded fund (ETF) — designed to track the performance of a market
index rather than to beat it. The S&P 500, the FTSE All-World, and the
Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index are typical underlyings. Instead of a
portfolio manager picking stocks, the fund mechanically holds the index
constituents in proportion to their index weights.

## Why they dominate

Three properties make index funds the default core holding for the bulk of
private wealth assets in most developed markets:

1. **Cost.** A broad-market equity index ETF charges 0.03% to 0.20% per
   year. The average actively-managed equity mutual fund charges around
   0.70%. Compounded over thirty years that fee gap consumes roughly 20%
   of an investor's terminal wealth.
2. **Performance.** SPIVA scorecards consistently show that 80%+ of
   actively-managed equity funds underperform their benchmark net of fees
   over a fifteen-year horizon. Picking the rare future winner ex-ante is
   extraordinarily hard.
3. **Tax efficiency.** ETFs in particular have low portfolio turnover and
   the in-kind creation/redemption mechanism limits capital gains
   distributions, which improves after-tax returns in taxable accounts.

## How to choose one

When evaluating an index fund, the relevant attributes are:

- **Total expense ratio (TER)** — lower is better, all else equal.
- **Tracking error / tracking difference** — does it actually deliver the
  index return after fees?
- **Replication method** — full physical (holds every constituent) is
  simplest; sampled physical is fine for very broad indices; synthetic
  (swap-based) introduces counterparty risk in exchange for tighter
  tracking on hard-to-replicate markets.
- **Fund size and trading volume** — larger ETFs have tighter bid-ask
  spreads and lower closure risk.
- **Domicile and tax treatment** — Irish-domiciled UCITS ETFs are common
  for European investors because of favourable withholding-tax treaties.

## Limitations

An index fund will never beat its index — by construction it tracks it.
Investors uncomfortable with that should be prepared to demonstrate
genuine, repeatable manager skill before paying for active management. In
narrow or inefficient markets (e.g. small-cap emerging market debt) an
active manager can sometimes add value, but in the deep, liquid markets
where most retail money sits — US large-cap equities, developed-market
government bonds — the empirical case for indexing is overwhelming.

For a wealth manager building a client portfolio, a small number of
broad-market index ETFs typically forms the strategic core, with active or
factor tilts at the margin if at all.
