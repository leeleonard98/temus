# Asset Allocation

Asset allocation is the decision about what fractions of a portfolio sit in
broad asset classes — equities, bonds, cash, real assets, alternatives —
and is widely regarded as the single most important driver of long-run
investment outcomes. The classic Brinson–Hood–Beebower studies found that
asset allocation explained roughly 90% of the variation in returns over
time across pension portfolios, dwarfing the contribution of security
selection or market timing.

## Strategic vs. tactical

Strategic asset allocation (SAA) is the long-term policy mix. It is set
based on the investor's goals, time horizon, and capacity for loss, and is
rebalanced back to target periodically. SAA changes slowly — perhaps every
few years as life circumstances change.

Tactical asset allocation (TAA) layers shorter-term tilts on top, over- or
under-weighting asset classes based on valuation, momentum, or macro
views. TAA can add value but consistently delivering it is hard, requires
discipline to take risk off when consensus is loudest, and amplifies the
behavioural risk of selling at the bottom.

For most private clients SAA does the heavy lifting; TAA tilts should be
modest and only employed if the underlying view has a clear evidentiary
basis.

## Common frameworks

A frequently quoted heuristic is **"100 minus your age in equities"**,
giving a 30-year-old 70% equities and a 70-year-old 30%. The rule is
crude — it ignores the size of other resources (pension, property), the
income gap between assets and goals, and the client's actual emotional
tolerance for drawdowns — but it captures the right direction: as the
horizon to the goal shortens, the portfolio should de-risk so a bad year
does not derail the plan.

More sophisticated approaches use:

- **Liability-driven investing (LDI)** — match the duration of bond
  holdings to the duration of future spending needs.
- **Goals-based allocation** — split the portfolio into sleeves
  (essential, lifestyle, aspirational) each with their own risk profile.
- **Risk parity** — weight asset classes so each contributes equal
  portfolio volatility, typically resulting in much higher bond allocations
  than market-cap weighting.

## Implementation

Once the policy mix is set, allocation is implemented through low-cost
broad-market vehicles in each sleeve. The granularity matters less than
the broad split — a portfolio with 60% global equities and 40% global
investment-grade bonds, held in two ETFs, will track a much fancier
twelve-fund version closely while costing a fraction as much.

The allocator's main job is then to ensure the portfolio is rebalanced
back to target as markets move it away, and to revisit the SAA when life
circumstances materially change.
